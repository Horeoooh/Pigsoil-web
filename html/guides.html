Day 1-21 Guide

Basic Swine Manure Composting

Complete 21-Day Step-by-Step Guide for Beginners

About This Method

Traditional aerobic composting using swine waste and plant matter. This beginner-friendly method creates high-quality organic fertilizer through natural decomposition processes. Perfect for small to medium-scale swine farms with minimal equipment requirements.

Materials Needed

Fresh swine manure (100-200 kg)
Dry plant matter/leaves
Rice hulls or sawdust
Shovel or pitchfork
Water source/hose
Tarpaulin cover
Thermometer (optional)
pH test kit (optional)
1
Initial Setup & Layering
Task: Layer swine manure & plant matter
Time Required: 2-3 hours

Start by selecting a well-drained area away from water sources and living areas. Create alternating layers of materials:

1. Start with a 6-inch layer of coarse plant material (corn stalks, rice straw)
2. Add 4 inches of fresh swine manure
3. Sprinkle 2 inches of dry materials (rice hulls, sawdust)
4. Repeat layering 3-4 times until pile reaches 1.2-1.5 meters high
5. Water each layer lightly as you build
Pro Tip:
Maintain a 3:1 ratio of brown materials (carbon-rich) to green materials (nitrogen-rich manure). The pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge - moist but not dripping.
7
First Turning
Task: Turn pile for aeration
Time Required: 1-2 hours

The first turning is crucial for maintaining proper oxygen levels:

1. Using a pitchfork or shovel, turn the entire pile
2. Move outside materials to the center and center materials to the outside
3. Break up any large clumps
4. Check moisture levels throughout the pile
5. Add water if needed to maintain proper moisture
6. Rebuild pile in same location
Pro Tip:
After turning, the pile temperature will drop temporarily but should rise again within 24-48 hours, indicating continued active decomposition.
14
Add Dry Carbon Materials
Task: Second turning and carbon addition
Time Required: 1.5-2 hours

Mid-process adjustment to balance the compost:

1. Turn the pile completely as done on day 7
2. Add additional dry carbon materials (rice hulls, dried leaves, sawdust)
3. Mix thoroughly to ensure even distribution
4. Adjust moisture if necessary
5. Check for proper brown-to-green ratio
6. Rebuild pile
Pro Tip:
This is a good time to adjust the carbon-nitrogen ratio if the pile has been too wet or smelly. Add more brown materials to balance excess nitrogen.
21
Final Turn & Screen Compost
Task: Final processing and screening
Time Required: 2-3 hours

Complete the composting process:

1. Perform final turning of the pile
2. Screen compost through 1/2 inch mesh to remove large particles
3. Return large particles to a new compost pile
4. Test pH (should be 6.5-7.5 for optimal use)
5. Check for proper moisture and crumbly texture
6. Package or store finished compost
7. Allow 1-2 weeks additional curing if needed
Final Quality Check:
Good compost should be dark brown, crumbly, have an earthy smell, and feel cool to the touch. If it's still warm or has strong odors, allow additional curing time.

Hot Composting Method

Complete 18-Day High-Temperature Composting Guide

THERMOPHILIC PROCESS (55-70°C)

About This Method

Advanced thermophilic composting that uses controlled high-temperature processes to rapidly decompose swine manure while ensuring complete pathogen elimination and weed seed destruction. This method maintains internal temperatures between 55-70°C for 15+ consecutive days, creating premium-quality, pathogen-free organic fertilizer in just 18 days.


Key Benefits: Faster processing, superior pathogen control, better nutrient retention, reduced odors, and professional-grade results.

Materials Needed

Fresh swine manure (150-250 kg)
High-carbon materials (sawdust, paper)
Compost thermometer (essential)
Pitchfork or aerator tool
Water source
Insulating cover/tarp
pH testing kit
Moisture meter (optional)
1
High-Energy Initial Build
Task: Create optimal thermophilic conditions
Time Required: 3-4 hours

Build the pile for maximum heat generation:

1. Choose a sheltered location with good drainage
2. Create a base layer of coarse materials (3 inches)
3. Mix fresh swine manure with high-carbon materials (2:1 ratio)
4. Build pile rapidly to minimum 1.5m x 1.5m x 1.5m (critical mass)
5. Ensure moisture content is 55-65% throughout
6. Insert thermometer into center of pile
7. Cover with insulating material
Target Temperature:
Expect pile to reach 40-50°C within 24-48 hours. Proper size and moisture are critical for heat generation.
4
First Hot Turn
Task: Turn pile for optimal heat distribution
Time Required: 1.5-2 hours

Critical first turning for even heating:

1. Record temperature before turning
2. Turn entire pile, moving outer materials to center
3. Ensure all materials reach thermophilic zone
4. Check moisture throughout and adjust as needed
5. Rebuild pile maintaining same dimensions
6. Re-insert thermometer in new center
7. Cover immediately to retain heat
Critical Point:
This turn is essential for pathogen kill. Every part of the materials must experience the high-heat zone. Temperature will drop temporarily but should recover within 24 hours.
11
Third Turn & Assessment
Task: Turn and assess decomposition progress
Time Required: 1.5-2 hours

Mid-process assessment and turning:

1. Document temperature before turning
2. Perform complete turn of all materials
3. Assess decomposition progress - materials should be darker
4. Check for proper texture (less fibrous, more uniform)
5. Test pH levels (should be approaching 6.5-7.5)
6. Adjust moisture if needed
7. Rebuild pile maintaining heat-retention structure
18
Harvest & Screen Final Product
Task: Final processing and harvest
Time Required: 2-3 hours

Complete the hot composting process:

1. Screen compost through 1/2 inch mesh
2. Remove any remaining large particles
3. Test final pH and moisture content
4. Package finished compost for storage or use
5. Return oversized materials to new compost batch
6. Document final yield and quality
7. Store in dry, ventilated area
Success Indicators:
Perfect hot compost is dark brown, crumbly, smells earthy, feels cool, and has uniform texture with no visible manure pieces.
